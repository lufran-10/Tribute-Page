Marie Curie

A timeline of her life

  • 1867 - Maria Salomea Skłodowska was born in Warsaw, Congress Poland (part of the Russian Empire), as the fifth and youngest child of a couple of well-known teachers.
  • 1890 - While she continued her studies at the Flying University, a clandestine Polish patriotic institution of higher learning that admitted women students, she began her practical scientific training in a chemical laboratory at the Museum of Industry and Agriculture, near Warsaw's Old Town. The laboratory was run by her cousin, who had been an assistant in Saint Petersburg to the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev. Through all her studies she continued working as a governess.
  • 1891 - She followed her elder sister to study in Paris at the Sorbonne, where she earned her higher degrees and conducted her subsequent scientific work.
  • 1895 - Marie married the French physicist Pierre Curie.
  • 1902 - She discovered pure radium.
  • 1903 - Marie shared the Nobel Prize in Physics with her husband and with the physicist Henri Becquerel for their pioneering work developing the theory of "radioactivity" — a term she coined.
  • 1904 - She was promoted to chief assistant to her husband in his laboratory.
  • 1906 - Pierre Curie died in Paris on a street accident. She took his place teaching at the Sorbonne and became the first woman professor there.
  • 1911 - She won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her discovery of the elements polonium and radium, using techniques she invented for isolating radioactive isotopes.
  • 1914 - During WWI, Marie used her radioactive research materials to do "x-rays". The x-rays were used to found bullets and other shrapnel in wounded soldiers.
  • 1920 - She founded the Curie Institute in Paris.
  • 1922 - Marie became a member of the French Academy of Medicine. She devoted herself to using her knowledge to help people.
  • 1932 - Marie and her sister started a research facility to help fight cancer. It is still open today and it's called the Marie Sklodowska Curie Oncology Center.
  • 1934 - She died at the age of 66 in France, of aplastic anemia from exposure to radiation during her scientific research and in the course of her radiological work at field hospitals during World War I.
